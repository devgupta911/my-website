The Universe

Neutron Star

A neutron star is the incredibly dense, compact remnant of a massive star that has undergone a supernova explosion. It is one of the most extreme and fascinating objects in the universe.

Formation

  • Massive Star Collapse: Neutron stars form from the core of a star that started its life with a mass between 8 to 25 times the mass of the Sun.
  • When the star exhausts its nuclear fuel, it can no longer support itself against gravity. The outer layers collapse and rebound in a supernova explosion, leaving behind the dense core.
  • If the core's mass is between about 1.4 and 3 solar masses, it compresses into a neutron star. If the mass exceeds this limit, it will form a black hole.

Black Hole

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so intense that nothing—not even light—can escape. They are among the most fascinating phenomena in the universe.

Formation

  • Stellar Collapse: A massive star (>25x Sun's mass) collapses into a stellar black hole.
  • Mergers: Collisions between neutron stars or black holes can form larger black holes.
  • Primordial Black Holes: Hypothetical black holes formed in the early universe.

Key Features

  • Event Horizon: The "point of no return" where nothing escapes.
  • Singularity: The center where gravity is infinitely strong.
  • Accretion Disk: A disk of gas and dust heated by gravitational forces.
  • Relativistic Jets: High-energy particle jets emitted along the axis of rotation.
